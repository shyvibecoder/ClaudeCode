## 0. The one-paragraph version

You asked to skate to where the puck will be, not where it is — and you named AI compute as where it *has* been. The honest finding of this study is uncomfortable: **most of "where the puck is going" is already crowded with skaters.** Nearly every scarcity the research surfaced — transformers, copper, rare-earth magnets, gallium, HBM, advanced packaging, optics, liquid cooling, uranium — is *downstream of a single driver* (AI-datacenter capex + electrification), and the marquee ways to play them have already run 2–5x and trade at 16–60x. The genuine edge is therefore **not** another long list of hot scarcities; it is four things: **(1)** owning the handful of bottlenecks that are *slow-to-build and can't be quadrupled in 24 months* — skilled electrical labor, grid interconnection, large transformers, copper, and heavy-rare-earth *separation*; **(2)** the contrarian *de-rated* quality names the crowd left for dead (life-science tools, enrichment-over-mining); **(3)** keeping dry powder for the >50%-likely 2027–2028 capex-growth air-pocket that will let you buy the *good* crowded chokepoints cheaper; and **(4)** accepting that for the very best scarcities there is **no clean ETF** — the alpha is held privately or foreign. Two iron laws govern all of it: **a chokepoint is not a rent** (Wolfspeed owned the GaN/SiC chokepoint and went bankrupt), and **the best chokepoints are inaccessible** (private/foreign/impaired).

## PART I — The single most important conclusion: it's one factor, not seven

The deep dives independently produced a long list of "structural scarcities." The red-teams independently produced a single, devastating observation: **they are overwhelmingly the same trade.**

Transformers, switchgear, gas turbines, HVDC cable, electricians, copper, HBM, CoWoS, optical interconnect, liquid cooling, SiC/GaN power, and the uranium-via-SMR narrative are **all derivatives of one independent variable: hyperscaler AI-datacenter capex + the broader electrification it accelerates.** That number is ~$600–690B in 2026. Its *growth rate* is what matters for these second-derivative trades, and on the published trajectory it decelerates hard:

| Year | Hyperscaler capex growth (Allianz/Moody's-style consensus) |
|---|---|
| 2026 | ~+51% |
| 2027 | ~+13% |
| 2028 | ~+5% |

**Implication:** a "growth air-pocket" — where the *level* stays high but the *growth* collapses — is nearly mechanical by 2027–2028, and that is exactly when memory/packaging/optics/power-equipment stocks (which are priced on growth, not level) de-rate. Early demand cracks are already visible: Microsoft cancelled ~200 MW of leases and let >1 GW of LOIs expire; only ~6.3 GW of US data centers are *under construction* versus ~21.5 GW *announced*; first data-center lease defaults are expected 2027–2028.

So the "diversified basket of seven scarcities" is, in a drawdown, **one correlated bet with a ~1.0 internal correlation.** Any portfolio built naively from the deep-dive lists is far less diversified than it looks. **This is the single biggest blind spot in the raw research, and it reshapes everything below.**

## PART III — The five cross-cutting meta-scarcities (the patterns that recur across every tech)

Strip away the sector labels and the *same five bottlenecks* appear in space, robotics, AI, energy, defense, and biotech. These are the real "where the puck is going," because they are demanded by *everything* at once:

1. **Electric power and everything between the generator and the chip.** Grid interconnection, large transformers, switchgear, and the grain-oriented electrical steel and bushings upstream of them. Demanded by AI, electrification, reshoring, defense, and robotics simultaneously. *Cannot be quadrupled in 24 months* (multi-year permitting) — which is exactly why it's more durable than the manufacturing scarcities.

2. **Skilled physical labor.** Electricians (Microsoft's self-described "#1 problem"), but also pipefitters, substation commissioning crews, drilling crews (geothermal), nuclear/SRM technicians, hermetic-packaging engineers, and rare-earth separation experts. **The one input that cannot be imported, automated, or capitalized away on this timescale.** Recurs in every single deep dive.

3. **Processing / refining / separation chokepoints (not the raw resource).** The lesson of critical minerals generalizes: the bottleneck and the moat both live in the *middle* of the value chain — heavy-RE separation, uranium enrichment, magnet-making, gallium refining, GMP viral-vector manufacturing, advanced semiconductor packaging. You can own the mine (or the molecule, or the qubit) and still be hostage to a separation/processing step someone else controls.

4. **Proprietary, hard-to-replicate data + the wet-lab/fleet capacity to generate it.** Robot manipulation data, RL/agentic environments, proprietary biological assay data. As models commoditize, the moat migrates to the data and the physical apparatus that produces it — and it *compounds* (whoever deploys first collects the data that lets them deploy better).

5. **Specialized, single-source materials and the gases/chemistries beneath them.** ABF film (Ajinomoto), EUV photoresist (Japan ~90%), He-3 (tritium decay), ammonium perchlorate (2 US suppliers), affinity resins for AAV. Quiet, geopolitically fragile, near-monopoly chemistry — the scarcities *underneath* the scarcities.

**The meta-pattern:** the durable scarcities are the **slow-to-build physical and human ones** (#1, #2, #3, #5), not the fast-ramp manufacturing ones (HBM, CoWoS, cooling) that capital is actively quadrupling. *Skate to the inputs that take a decade and a permit to build, not the ones that take a fab and 18 months.*

## PART IV — Two iron laws that break naive scarcity investing

The red-teams surfaced two principles that invalidate the simple "find the chokepoint, buy the chokepoint" logic. Internalize these or the rest is dangerous.

### Law 1 — A chokepoint is not a rent.
**Wolfspeed owned the SiC/GaN substrate chokepoint and filed Chapter 11 in June 2025**, wiping out ~95% of equity. Being the bottleneck supplier does *not* guarantee pricing power: capital intensity, Chinese oversupply collapsing prices, and a leveraged balance sheet can take the equity to zero *while the scarcity is completely real*. Harmonic Drive holds a near-monopoly in precision reducers and is being *margin-compressed* by Chinese entrants (Leaderdrive revenue +47% in 2025). **China oversupply is the universal solvent** — it broke SiC pricing, is breaking reducers, and sets the rare-earth price floor that forced the US government to subsidize MP Materials. *The scarcity can be real and the equity still go to zero.* Always ask: does the chokepoint owner actually capture the rent, or get competed/leveraged into the ground?

### Law 2 — The best chokepoints are inaccessible.
A recurring, frustrating finding: the *genuinely* scarce assets are
- **private** (SpaceX, Anduril, Physical Intelligence, Skild, Scale AI, Riverlane, Acuitas/Genevant, Noveon), or
- **foreign and awkward to hold** (Harmonic Drive & Nabtesco in Tokyo, Leaderdrive in Shenzhen, Lynas in Australia, SK Hynix in Korea, ASML in the Netherlands, Hitachi Energy in Japan, Ajinomoto/Shin-Etsu/JSR), or
- **impaired** (Wolfspeed, Catalent).

So the public/ETF investor is repeatedly funneled into either (a) over-owned momentum proxies (RKLB, VRT, MP, ASTS), or (b) thematic ETFs whose top holdings are the same megacaps already in any S&P fund. **For the scarcities that are most genuinely scarce, there is often no clean public expression — the scarcity alpha is captured by private/VC/foreign holders.** This is not a reason to give up; it's a reason to be deliberate about the *few* clean public expressions that do exist (Part VI) and to consider private/pre-IPO vehicles for the rest.

## PART V — Where the puck ALREADY is vs. where it's genuinely GOING

**Already there (crowded; right thesis, late/expensive entry — own only on a de-rating):**
GE Vernova, Vertiv, Micron, the optics duo (Coherent/Lumentum), MP Materials, Cameco/uranium miners, Rocket Lab, AST SpaceMobile. These *tripled-to-quintupled* on the exact narrative this study would generate. The asymmetry is now to the downside; several trade above sell-side price targets.

**Genuinely still under-owned / non-consensus (per the red-teams):**
1. **Skilled electrical labor / EPC** — the one un-importable, un-automatable bottleneck (PWR, EME, MYRG, FIX). *Verify it hasn't itself become crowded — see Part VII.*
2. **Enrichment over mining** — Centrus/LEU is the actual fuel-cycle chokepoint with a hard 2028 cliff, less owned than the CCJ/URA miner crowd (though not cheap).
3. **De-rated quality life-science tools** — Danaher, Thermo Fisher, Illumina at ~20x after a brutal 2021–25 de-rate (ILMN −70% from peak). The *real* picks-and-shovels, hated and un-crowded — the contrarian inverse of the consensus trades.
4. **GMP viral-vector / reshored biologics capacity** — Lonza, Charles River (tight supply, reshoring tailwind; *avoid Catalent — impaired*).
5. **The single-source materials layer** — ABF/photoresist/specialty-gas names (foreign), and grid components the crowd skipped (switchgear, GOES).
6. **Copper, as the slowest and most certain long-duration deficit** — not a 2026 trade; a 2030–35 inevitability to accumulate into weakness.

**A sharp warning on recency bias:** this study was built in May 2026 from 2025–26 sources. Its instinct to rate the things that already 3x'd (uranium, MP, GEV) as "high conviction" and the things that *haven't* run (quantum, biotech tools) as "low-prob optionality" is **recency, not probability.** A 2021-vintage version of this exact method would have over-weighted ARKK, hydrogen, and SPACs. The discipline that protects you is *price paid*, not narrative quality.

## PART VI — What to actually do: ETFs and expressions, ranked by probability

You asked for ETFs and the high-probability bets. Here is the honest mapping — *revised after a final adversarial pass that caught me over-correcting into un-actionable "everything's priced, wait" mush.* Two corrections changed the recommendation: **(1) "went up a lot" is not the same as "expensive"** — Micron rose ~239% in 2025 *and* trades at ~10–12x **forward** earnings with HBM4 sold out for all of 2026; that's cheap, not rich. **(2) "Wait for a 2027–28 de-rating" is itself the consensus fear** (Allianz, et al.), and Barclays argues capex estimates are ~$225B *too low* for 2027–28 with the peak in 2028 — so the pain trade could be *up*, and parking in cash front-runs a crowded bear. **Conclusion: deploy now, weighted to backlog-protected and forward-cheap — don't try to time the air-pocket.**

**The uncomfortable structural headline still stands: for the most robust scarcities there is no clean ETF — they need single names.** ETFs are the second-best, more-diversified expression.

### Tier 1 — Deploy now: backlog-protected or forward-cheap (visibility *through* the feared digestion)

Scale in; pair with the cheaper electron expression below.
  - **Merchant power / IPPs — Constellation, Vistra, Talen** — own the electrons, not just the equipment. The study's biggest earlier omission; arguably cleaner than the turbine multiple.
  - ETF proxies: **NUKZ** / **NLR** (capture nuclear utilities + IPPs + reactor builders), **GRID** (grid/electrical equipment — Eaton, GEV, ABB, Quanta).

Cyclical (3 suppliers adding ~50% capacity → memory always overshoots both ways), so *size for volatility* — but on risk-adjusted forward value this is the name the first draft wrongly told you to avoid. No clean ETF isolates it; **SMH/SOXX** hold it small.
- **EME (EMCOR) over PWR (Quanta)** — same electrical-build scarcity, but EME ~26x vs PWR ~48–54x (priced for perfection, ~280% above its industry median). Prefer the reasonably-priced expression of an identical thesis.

### Tier 2 — Own as structural holds, but recognize they're now crowded thesis-positions (not contrarian)
- **COPX** (Copper Miners, ER 0.65%) — the slowest, most-certain, longest-duration deficit, *and* the one with genuine **non-AI** demand (global grid replacement, electrification ex-datacenter) — so it's the closest thing to a real de-correlator. But copper is at all-time highs with JPM/UBS targets rising; this is a *thesis position at a high entry*, not a quiet contrarian one. Accumulate on weakness; tilt some to laggards (COPJ).
- **GRID / PAVE** — core "physical build-out" exposure. PAVE's breadth is the built-in hedge against single-name multiple risk. On-thesis and durable, though not cheap.

Size small; this is no longer the under-owned trade the first draft implied.
- **SMH** — acknowledge it's **~30% Nvidia+TSMC = AI-GPU beta**, not chokepoint alpha. Fine as core AI exposure. The real semicap monopoly (ASML) and HBM (Micron/SK Hynix) are single names.

### Tier 3 — Genuine de-correlators + call-options (the *only* things that don't share the AI-capex factor)
*Because Tier 1–2 are all downstream of one driver (see Part VII.D), these are where real diversification lives — size them as such.*

### The de-rated-tools call — downgraded after the final pass
DHR/TMO/ILMN at ~20x looked like the clean contrarian inverse — but the Street is **already re-rating tools *up* in 2026**, so the easy re-rate has started (you're late, not early), and ILMN specifically carries falling-knife risk. Keep it as a *small* value-patience position, not a high-conviction call. This was the weakest leg and I'm flagging it as such.

### No-clean-ETF scarcities (single-stock or private only) — still the *most robust*

### The posture (revised: deploy-now barbell with a trigger, not a cash pile)

Without a trigger, "wait" becomes permanent — that was the first draft's error.
- **Sleeve C — de-correlators + options (~10–15%):** the non-AI and early-stage names above — the only genuine diversification from the single factor.

## PART VIII — Bottom line

- **The biggest risk in your own framing** ("be where the puck will be") is that the research-driven answer is *already* a crowded, richly-priced consensus — you're asking at the top of the cycle that created the scarcity. The crowd is the position. *But the inverse error is just as costly:* refusing to own a secular grid/copper/power deficit because it "already ran" leaves you in cash while it compounds. Don't time it; own the right form of it.
- **The durable edge is the slow-to-build inputs** — electrons/power, grid, skilled labor, processing/separation, proprietary data, single-source materials — *not* the fast-ramp manufacturing scarcities being actively quadrupled (HBM capacity, CoWoS, cooling). Skate to what takes a decade and a permit to build.
- **Two laws keep you safe:** a chokepoint is not a rent (Wolfspeed went bankrupt owning the GaN/SiC chokepoint); the best chokepoints are inaccessible (private/foreign/impaired) — so for the most robust scarcities there is no clean ETF.
- **The single highest-conviction, most-actionable idea** (buried in the first draft, surfaced by the adversary): **own the electrons** — power generation with multi-year booked backlog (GE Vernova, Siemens Energy) and the merchant-power IPPs (Constellation, Vistra, Talen) — because it's the purest slow-to-build scarcity *and* a backlog that pays you *through* the digestion you're afraid of. Pair it with **Micron** (forward-cheap, HBM sold out) and **EME** (the reasonably-priced electrical-build name).
